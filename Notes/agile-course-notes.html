Traditional Software Delivery (Waterfall Model)

Waterfall Process

üíß Sequential Development Phases

  1. Requirements: Define problem, identify scope, develop plan/strategy
  2. Analysis: Investigate problem to define necessary requirements ("what")
  3. Design: Design solution based on requirements ("how")
  4. Code: Implement (program) the design
  5. Test: Test program to ensure requirements are satisfied
  6. Maintenance: Fix or improve applications based on use or environment changes

üìã Waterfall Characteristics

  • Full project planning upfront
  • Huge time to deliver
  • Customer not involved
  • Hard to accommodate change requests
  • Deadline creep (phase delays lead to whole project delays)

Advantages & Disadvantages

‚úÖ Advantages

  • Each phase must be completed before the next
  • Suited for smaller projects where requirements are well-defined
  • Quality assurance tests (Verification and Validation) performed before completing each stage
  • Elaborate documentation done at every phase
  • Changes in software made during development process

‚ùå Disadvantages

  • Errors can only be fixed during the phase they occur
  • Not desirable for complex projects where requirements change frequently
  • Testing occurs quite late in the development process
  • Documentation occupies a lot of developer and tester time
  • Project completely dependent on project team with minimum client intervention
  • Clients' valuable feedback cannot be included with ongoing development
  • Small changes or errors in completed software may cause many problems

üìú Historical Note

Origin: Supposedly "invented" by Dr. Winston W. Royce's 1970 paper "Managing the Development of Large Software Systems." However, Royce's paper identified the deficiencies of this pattern and proposed enhancements, stating: "I believe in this concept, but the implementation described above is risky and invites failure."

12 Agile Principles

1 Highest priority is to satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery of valuable software.
2 Welcome changing requirements, even late in development; harness change for customer's competitive advantage.
3 Deliver working software frequently, from a couple of weeks to a couple of months, with a preference for shorter timescales.
4 Business people and developers must work together daily throughout the project.
5 Build projects around motivated individuals. Give them the environment and support they need, and trust them to get the job done.
6 The most efficient and effective method of conveying information is face-to-face conversation.
7 Working software is the primary measure of progress.
8 Agile processes promote sustainable development. Sponsors, developers, and users should maintain a constant pace indefinitely.
9 Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design enhances agility.
10 Simplicity ‚Äì the art of maximizing the amount of work not done ‚Äì is essential.
11 The best architectures, requirements, and designs emerge from self-organizing teams.
12 At regular intervals, the team reflects on how to become more effective, then tunes and adjusts its behavior accordingly.

Iterative & Incremental Development

üîÑ Iterative
  • Develops through repeated cycles
  • Starts simple, expected to change
  • Used to find the right solution
  • Fail early to improve candidate solution
üìà Incremental
  • Develops smaller portions at a time
  • Gradually builds up functionality
  • Allows value to be delivered early
  • Each increment adds working features

Lean Methodology

üè≠ Toyota Production System (TPS)

Toyota engineers Shigeo Shingo and Taiichi Ohno, inspired by Henry Ford and Frederick Winslow Taylor, sought to eliminate waste by scaling back production to bare essentials ("lean") since 1934.

Overall Objective: Limit resources to only what is needed.

Lean Management Pillars

üíé Delivering Value to Customer

Customer value is the sum of all activities a customer pays for because they add value to a product or service.

üóëÔ∏è Eliminating Waste

Eliminate anything that doesn't add value from the customer's perspective.

üîÑ Continuous Improvement

Through constant experimentation, promoting small changes, and knowledge sharing, cultivating a culture of "there is always a better way."

üéØ Five Lean Principles

  1. Define value from the customer perspective
  2. Identify value stream - activities necessary to create the product
  3. Implement flow principle - process optimization in all departments
  4. Introduce pull principle - only produce what customer ordered
  5. Strive for perfection - zero-error principle, continuous improvement

Cross-Functional Teams

üè¢ Functional Teams
  • Groups from same functional areas
  • Traditional coordination
  • Innovation only within function
  • Long cycle times due to handoffs
ü§ù Cross-Functional Teams
  • Representatives from various functions
  • Improved coordination
  • Increased innovation in product and process
  • Reduced cycle times for customer touch points

Tuckman Model - Stages of Group Development

1. Forming

Questioning, socializing, eagerness, focusing on group identity

2. Storming

Resistance, conflict, competition, high emotions

3. Norming

Reconciliation, lowered anxiety, developing cohesion

4. Performing

Interdependence, healthy system, effective team production

5. Adjourning

Process orientation, recognition of team efforts

üìà Leadership Strategies by Stage

  • Forming: Take the lead, provide clear expectations/instructions
  • Storming: Normalize matters, encourage leadership
  • Norming: Recognize efforts, provide learning opportunities
  • Performing: Celebrate, guide from the side, encourage decision-making
  • Adjourning: Recognize change, provide summative evaluations

Scrum Artifacts

üéØ Product Goal

A long-term objective for the product

üìã Product Backlog

Emergent, ordered list of what's needed to improve the product. Includes Epics, Features, User Stories, Tasks, and Bugs.

üéØ Sprint Goal

Objective for the Sprint, met through Product Backlog implementation. Should be specific and measurable.

üìù Sprint Backlog

Set of Product Backlog items selected for Sprint, plus plan for delivering Increment and realizing Sprint Goal.

üì¶ Increment

Sum of usable Sprint Backlog items completed that meet Definition of Done, plus value of all prior Increments.

üöÄ MVP

Minimum Viable Product - product with just enough features to satisfy early customers and provide feedback.

Scrum Events (Ceremonies)

üìÖ Sprint Planning

Timebox: Max 8 hours for month-long Sprint

  • Decide what work to complete
  • Discuss implementation approach
  • Determine committed items
  • Fine-tune sprint goal
üèÉ Daily Scrum

Timebox: Max 15 minutes

  • Inspect progress toward Sprint Goal
  • Same time and place daily
  • All team members attend
  • Adapt Sprint Backlog as needed
üëÅÔ∏è Sprint Review

Timebox: Max 4 hours for month-long Sprint

  • Inspect Sprint outcome
  • Present results to stakeholders
  • Discuss progress toward Product Goal
  • Get feedback on product and backlog
üîÑ Sprint Retrospective

Timebox: Max 3 hours for month-long Sprint

  • Reflect on how to become more effective
  • Inspect how iteration was done
  • Decide on process improvements
  • Incorporate improvements in next iteration

üîÑ Backlog Refinement

Ongoing activity to add details, estimates, and order to items in the product backlog. Not a formal event but continuous collaborative effort.

Key Definitions

Definition of Ready (DoR) & Definition of Done (DoD)

‚úÖ Definition of Ready (DoR)

Explicit criteria a user story must meet before acceptance into an iteration (often based on INVEST matrix).

  • User story has business value
  • Estimated by development team
  • Clear and well-defined
  • Achievable within a sprint
  • Measurable and testable
  • Dependencies identified
üèÅ Definition of Done (DoD)

All conditions a software product must satisfy to be accepted by user, customer, team, or consuming system.

  • Code completion
  • Unit tests checked and green
  • Functional tests passed
  • QA done/issues resolved
  • All team members approve work
  • No pending work in any stage
  • CI/TDD verified

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Rule

"90% DONE is NOT DONE." Avoid unfinished work at the end of a sprint.

Task Switching (Context Switching)

üß† Cost of Context Switching

  • Consumes time and energy
  • Every switch has a cognitive cost
  • Every context switch restarts the cycle
  • Fragmented multi-tasking kills productivity

üìä Impact Statistics

  • A 6-month task could take 3.6 months without context loss
  • 20% time lost with 2 simultaneous tasks
  • 75% time lost with 5 simultaneous tasks
  • Our brains are designed to focus on one thing at a time

Key Principle: "ONE THING AT A TIME!"

Kanban System

üìã Kanban Overview

Origin: Japanese word "kanban" meaning "visual board" or "sign"

System Type: Pull system scheduling system

Kanban Board: Visualizes workflow with cards representing work items and columns representing workflow steps (e.g., Stories, To Do, In Progress, Testing, Done)

Kanban Practices

üëÅÔ∏è Visualize Workflow

Use a board with cards and columns to represent the actual state of the workflow, risks, and specifications. Make work visible to everyone.

üöß Limit Work in Progress (WIP)

Ensure a manageable number of active items are in progress. Set maximum items per stage so cards are only "pulled" when capacity is available.

üåä Manage Flow

Focus on managing the work, not the people. Achieve predictable and sustainable movement of work items through the production process.

üî¨ Improve Collaboratively

Use models and the scientific method. Cultivate organizational culture of continuous improvement through evolutionary change where hypotheses are proven.

üìã Make Process Policies Explicit

The process should be clearly defined, published, and socialized so everyone understands how work flows through the system.

üîÑ Implement Feedback Loops

Kanban suggests cadences at team level and service-oriented cadences for continuous improvement and synchronization.

üë• Team-level Cadence

Daily Team Kanban Meeting for tracking status and work flow

üéØ Service-oriented Cadences

Operations, service delivery, and risk meetings to synchronize and improve service delivery

Little's Law

üßÆ Little's Law (John Little, MIT, 1961)

Cycle Time = WIP / Throughput
‚è±Ô∏è Cycle Time

Average time an item spends in the system (e.g., user stories, coffee orders, hospital patients)

üì¶ WIP (Work in Process)

Average number of items in the system at any given time

üöÄ Throughput

Rate at which items move through (or are processed by) the system on average

üìà Key Principles

  • "Start less, to finish more" - If the goal is to complete work sooner, starting more work is the worst course of action
  • Stability: In a stable system, items enter and exit at the same rate; all items entering will exit
  • Resource Utilization: High resource utilization can lead to long queues

‚òï Starbucks Example

Scenario: 11 people in line, 1 person served per minute

Result: 11 minutes to get coffee

Lesson: High resource utilization can lead to long queues and wait times

Common Agile Anti-Patterns

üö´ Things to Avoid

  • Agile in Name Only: Following ceremonies without embracing values
  • Skipping Retrospectives: Missing opportunities for continuous improvement
  • Waterfall Planning: Over-planning sprints and features upfront
  • Ignoring Customer Feedback: Not incorporating user input into development
  • Blame Culture: Focusing on who rather than what and why
  • Feature Factory: Measuring success by output rather than outcomes
  • Lack of Technical Excellence: Neglecting code quality and technical debt

Agile Metrics & Measurement

Key Agile Metrics

üìà Velocity

Amount of work a team completes during a sprint, measured in story points or hours

  • Helps with sprint planning
  • Shows team capacity trends
  • Not for team comparison
üî• Burndown Chart

Visual representation of work remaining versus time in a sprint

  • Shows progress toward sprint goal
  • Identifies potential issues early
  • Helps with scope management
üìä Cumulative Flow Diagram

Shows the flow of work items through different stages over time

  • Identifies bottlenecks
  • Shows WIP levels
  • Predicts delivery dates
‚ö° Lead Time & Cycle Time

Time from request to delivery (Lead) vs. time from start to completion (Cycle)

  • Measures process efficiency
  • Helps set expectations
  • Identifies improvement opportunities

Business Value Metrics

üí∞ Value-Focused Measurements

  • Customer Satisfaction: Regular feedback and NPS scores
  • Business Value Delivered: Features that impact key business metrics
  • Time to Market: Speed of delivering new features to customers
  • Quality Metrics: Defect rates, customer-reported issues
  • Employee Engagement: Team satisfaction and retention rates
  • Innovation Rate: New ideas generated and implemented
